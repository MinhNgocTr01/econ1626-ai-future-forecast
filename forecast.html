When Productivity Is Not Enough

Forecasting AI's reshaping of the creative economy.

ECON1626 · Submitted May 2026 · ~2,200 words
01 — Thesis

The wrong assumption

I think the debate around AI begins with a flawed assumption: that productivity and value are the same thing.

Historically, that assumption has worked reasonably well. Most technologies create value by allowing society to produce more with less. AI appears to follow the same pattern, reducing costs, scaling knowledge, and automating cognitive labor at unprecedented speed. If output is all that matters, then the economic case for AI is straightforward.

The creative economy is where this logic begins to break down.

Part of the problem is that we often speak about "art" as though it serves a single purpose. It does not. Some creative work is instrumental. Advertising, commercial design, corporate writing, and background music are ultimately evaluated by their effectiveness. In these domains, AI is a powerful substitute because the value is largely embedded in the outcome.

Expressive work operates according to a different logic. We do not turn to literature, music, or art simply because they perform a function. We turn to them because they allow one consciousness to reach across time and circumstance in an attempt to be understood by another. The creator is not separate from the work. The creator is part of the value being consumed.

This is what makes generative AI culturally significant. For the first time, society is confronting a technology capable of reproducing the appearance of human expression without the human experience that traditionally gave that expression meaning. As synthetic content becomes abundant and increasingly indistinguishable from human created work, the central question is no longer whether AI can make art. It is whether authorship itself remains economically and culturally valuable once expression can exist without an expressive mind behind it.

Which one feels okay to be made by AI?

Most readers pick A. The instrumental / expressive split is intuitive once it has a name.

02 — Literature Review

Two stories that contend

The emerging literature on AI and creative work tells two competing stories. The first treats AI as an extension of human creativity. McKinsey (2023) and Goldman Sachs (2023) describe a productivity tool that lowers the cost of drafting while leaving meaning anchored in human intention. Erickson's (2024) case studies find that generative systems make production more labour-intensive, not less, while hiding human contributions behind an "AI-made" surface. The concern, on this view, is reorganisation, not replacement.

The second strand takes collapse seriously. Critics, ethicists, and the UNCTAD (2024) report argue that AI approximates style but cannot reproduce the lived experience that gives expressive work its meaning. Authenticity and provenance become economic, not just aesthetic, variables: when audiences care who made something, machine substitution changes the thing being produced.

My prior work (Tran 2025) applied the first framework to general labour markets. This essay argues the second is closer to right for creative work, and that no existing forecast traces what that means quarter by quarter through 2030.

The capability picture is consistent across all three domains. Midjourney V8, FLUX 2, GPT Image 1.5, and Imagen 4 produce photorealistic output indistinguishable from human work in most casual contexts. Suno v5.5 and Udio generate vocals and instrumentation most listeners cannot reliably identify as AI in blind tests. Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.5, and Gemini 3 produce long-form prose often indistinguishable from human writing in commercial contexts.

The labour data is messier than the capability data. California lost 14% of creative jobs (114,000 roles) between 2022 and 2025, but the Otis College report found the losses concentrated in film and traditional media while AI-exposed occupations were actually growing in job postings. The mechanism is disputed, and that dispute is itself evidence of the diagnostic confusion the instrumental / expressive distinction is meant to resolve.

On policy, EU AI Act Article 50 enforcement began in 2026, requiring machine-readable labeling of AI content in European markets. C2PA has been adopted by Adobe, Microsoft, Google, Sony, Canon, and Nikon. Whether this reaches consumers is the open question. This is where the forecast starts.

04 — The Forecast

Now to mid-2027

Q3 2026 The Disclosure Threshold

EU enforcement begins. Because platforms operate globally, the labeling rule reaches everyone. C2PA-verified human work begins commanding higher prices on stock platforms. This is the first test of whether people who say they value human-made work actually choose it when they can see the difference. If they do, a premium forms. If they keep clicking regardless, the label means nothing. Writing is hardest to label: a writer who edits every sentence of AI output and one who prompts and publishes are both "AI-assisted," and no regulation can tell them apart.

Q4 2026 The Copyright Settlement Wave

The Sony Music v. Suno ruling lands. OpenAI cases reach discovery. The Anthropic settlement ($1.5 billion, approximately $3,000 per work) sets the going rate. A licensing wave follows: rights holders who win sell their catalogues as training data; those who lose build their own AI products, Disney's $1 billion OpenAI deal being the clearest example. But the money goes to publishers, labels, and studios, not creators. The illustrator whose portfolio trained a model sees none of the $3,000. Where the money lands matters more than how much there is.

Q1 2027 Music Reaches the Tipping Point

AI music becomes indistinguishable in most popular genres, and the AI share of uploads could pass 50% on unfiltered platforms. This is the moment one major platform, likely Deezer, separates AI tracks from human ones, either into a different library or behind a verification requirement.

Q2 2027 Writing Approaches the Tipping Point

AI long-form text becomes hard to distinguish in genre fiction and commercial work. Writing differs from music and visual art in one way: the harm is invisible. A client never announces the switch, so the displacement is real but unseen. And what no one sees, no one protests. Upwork shows it anyway: writing projects fell 32% year-on-year into 2025. Routine work (SEO, product descriptions) is automating; specialist work (investigative journalism, literary fiction) holds and sometimes pays more. The instrumental / expressive split is showing up in prices.

05 — The Fork

Mid-2027

By mid-2027, AI produces work indistinguishable from human work in all three domains, and EU labeling has been in force a full year. The decisive question is what the US does next: follow with disclosure and provenance rules, or treat AI content like most commercial speech, with little federal regulation.

It is the same kind of fork the AI 2027 scenario describes, turning on who holds power in Washington, what they lose if regulation slows AI, and how visible the damage has become. At bottom it is whether American institutions build the language to tell instrumental work from expressive work, and whether enough powerful people believe the distinction is worth protecting.

Path A · Regulated
A managed outcome.
The US passes rules. Verified-human certification spreads. A smaller, more stratified creative class survives around verified authorship, curation, and live performance.
Path B · Market
A remnant economy.
The US declines to act. Platforms become producers. Median creator income collapses. The expressive layer survives as a niche, funded by philanthropy and private wealth.
06 — Path A

The Regulated Path, 2027–2030

Sage line

The US passes rules comparable to the EU AI Act: machine-readable labels, platform liability for unlabeled commercial AI content, and a framework for paying creators when their work trains models, modeled loosely on music royalties.

Q3 2027 Verified-human certification spreads

Led by publishing, where the Authors Guild has been building infrastructure for years; music and visual art follow. Content Credentials become recognizable the way "organic" did on food labels. The certification does not promise the work is good, only that a human made it. For the first time, the instrumental / expressive difference has formal recognition: platforms can show it, consumers can choose on it, creators can charge for it.

Q4 2027 The bottleneck shifts

The technical question is essentially closed. AI generates indistinguishable work at industrial scale. The scarce thing is no longer making something but knowing what is worth making and for whom. Curators, editors, and directors with reputations for taste become valuable again. The tools handle execution; what remains is deciding what to make, for whom, and why.

2028–2030 A new equilibrium

The creative economy is smaller and more divided between high-end verified human work and cheap automated content, but functional. A working creative class survives, not superstars or hobbyists but specialists in verified authorship, curation, and live performance. Median author income stops falling without returning to 2022 levels. Human artists' royalties are protected by a separate pool, and illustrators find verified work commands a premium in luxury and editorial markets, for those who can reach them. Path A is not a happy ending. It is a managed outcome, where losses are spread and the people still working are fewer but not gone.

07 — Path B

The Market Path, 2027–2030

Rust line

The US passes no federal rules. State laws (California's SB 942, Tennessee's ELVIS Act) cover too little to cohere. C2PA stays voluntary. Open-source models, recompilable without watermarking, break whatever labeling the commercial providers build.

Q3 2027 The preference gap widens

NPR and Luminate found people react negatively to AI music when they know it is AI. But streaming data tells a different story: when the algorithm picks the next song, the listener rarely faces a choice at all. This is not irrationality but design. A premium cannot form if the consumer is never asked.

Q4 2027 Platforms become producers

AI agents sustain creative projects lasting weeks. Streaming and publishing platforms stop being middlemen: a music service need not pay artists if it can generate songs per listener in real time; a publisher need not acquire manuscripts if it can produce genre fiction to order at near-zero cost. The working creative middle (illustrators, session musicians, mid-list authors, freelance journalists) loses its place entirely.

2028–2030 A remnant economy

Median creator income collapses. The Authors Guild's $23,329 full-time figure is roughly halved in real terms. Stock illustration, concept art, commercial design, and routine writing are nearly fully automated. Mid-tier musicians find live performance the only reliable income, but performance needs an audience, and audiences are built through recorded work now competing with infinite free alternatives. What remains concentrates in a few platforms and IP holders with old catalogues to train on and channels to distribute through.

The expressive layer does not vanish. But it is no longer an economy. It is a remnant.

08 — Policy

Markets, information, and power

The choices that determine the path are about markets, information, and power more than about AI.

Provenance disclosure

Gives consumers real information. The unresolved question is what counts as AI-generated: drawn too broadly, it stigmatizes the 45% of Authors Guild members already using AI tools; too narrowly, the label is meaningless.

Training-data compensation

Addresses collective action. Individuals cannot negotiate with OpenAI; collective licensing, like ASCAP and BMI for performance rights, could internalize the externality. The unintended consequence: smaller AI firms cannot afford licensing, accelerating concentration in AI development itself.

Platform regulation

Addresses the music problem. If algorithms decide what listeners hear and provenance stays buried in metadata, the market never transmits preference. The trade-off is platform autonomy and the difficulty of enforcing interface design across jurisdictions.

Public funding

Treats expressive work as a public good. The argument is not that the market failed but that it was never built to value what expressive work produces: a shared symbolic vocabulary, a record of what it felt like to be human at a moment. The trade-off is the politics of subsidy and the risk that funding shapes what gets made.

None suffices alone; none is costless. The question is which combination, at what moment, is implementable by institutions slower than the technology they govern.

09 — Uncertainty

What I do not know

This is an observed trajectory, not a model. The baseline is real but uneven: Spotify's payout is verifiable, Deezer's 34% is self-reported, the Otis job data is documented but its attribution disputed, and the Authors Guild and Upwork figures point the same way while measuring different things. Where data conflicts, I note it rather than resolve it.

The capability forecast draws on METR's autonomous-task-duration benchmark and the AI 2027 method of extrapolating from quantifiable progress. Both have limits. Capability curves do not always extrapolate smoothly, and there may be walls in audio coherence, narrative consistency, and emotional texture that are harder to scale than current trends suggest. I do not know where they are.

The deepest uncertainty is conceptual. The instrumental / expressive distinction is my framing, and it is contestable: what counts as expressive is socially constructed and may shift. If people raised on AI music do not value human origin as older listeners do, the provenance premium never forms, regardless of policy. I do not have a strong argument against this. I have a hope against it, which is different.

On disclosure: this essay was developed with AI assistance at every stage; the final writing and all original claims are mine. I disclose this as evidence, not caveat. Writing about AI's impact on writing, with AI as a collaborator, is the most direct illustration I have of the ideation/execution distinction at my argument's centre. AI helped me think. It did not think for me. Whether that distinction stays meaningful, and whether the reader can tell, is exactly what this forecast is about.

10 — Closing

The creative sector is not peripheral to the AI economy. It is where the economy's self-understanding is produced. Stories, images, and music are not merely outputs of civilization; they are how civilization thinks about itself.

The forecast predicts neither catastrophe nor salvation, but a fork and two plausible worlds beyond it. In one, the institutions around creative work learn to tell instrumental from expressive fast enough to preserve something worth keeping. In the other, they do not, and we learn what it means to live in a world more productive, more efficient, and somehow less inhabited.

The question is not whether AI will reshape the creative economy. It already has. The question is whether we will notice what we are losing before it is gone, or only after.
